Legal Alert:
Competition Watchdog Publishes the Retail Trade Code of Practice in the Wake of Landmark Ruling



Esteemed Client,

On 11 June 2021, the Competition Authority of Kenya (CAK) gazetted the Retail Trade Code of Practice (“the Code”). This was in response to arising issues in the retail sector which had also necessitated the amendment of the Competition Act to provide for ‘Abuse of Buyer Power’ as well as the publication of the Buyer Power Guidelines.

 

The vigilance of the new Buyer Power Department of the Competition Authority has been witnessed in the recent past where it investigated the conduct of Majid Al Futtaim Hypermarkets Limited (operator of the Carrefour brand) after a complaint was received from Orchards Limited, one of the hypermarket’s suppliers. The Authority found that the hypermarket had abused its buyer power, a decision that was upheld by the Competition Tribunal on appeal.

 

(See: Majid Al Futtaim Hypermarkets Limited and the CAK and Orchards Limited).

 

The Code, whose scope covers suppliers of goods to the Kenyan market regardless of their geographical location, has the following salient provisions:
  • Principle of Fair Dealing

    The Code requires retailers and suppliers to deal with one another fairly and lawfully, without duress, and in recognition of the other’s need for certainty as regards the costs and risks of trading. The Code also recognises that there may be ‘ordinary commercial pressures’ which may necessitate a retailer calling for certain actions by the supplier. Such pressures would be regarded as ‘ordinary’ only if they do not involve any form of duress, are objectively justifiable and transparent. The Code also notes the place of confidentiality and states that trade confidentialities shared between a supplier and a retailer in the normal line of business are not to be disclosed except upon the lapse of a year from the date they were revealed, or when required under law for purposes for dispute settlement.


  • Recording and Variation of Supply Agreements/ Joint Business Plans and Terms of Supply

    The Code requires that Supply Agreements/Joint Business Plans be recorded and that they include the following: terms of payment; payment date; interest payable on late payment; and mechanism for dispute resolution. Any variation of the agreements must be mutually agreed or otherwise preceded by notice and is not to apply retroactively unless the criteria in the Code are met.


  • Payments

    A retailer is expected to pay as agreed in the Supply Agreement and any anomalies in the documents must be notified to the supplier within 7 days or in the case of fresh and perishable goods, within 24 hours. Where there is a delay in payment, the retailer is to make the payment inclusive of interest at the rate agreed on in the supply agreement.

    The Code provides that unless expressly provided in the supply agreement, a retailer is precluded from requiring a supplier to pay its (supplier’s) costs with regard to category buyer visits to new or prospective suppliers; artwork or packaging design; consumer or market research; the opening or refurbishing of a store; hospitality for that retailer’s staff; and listing a product.

    A supplier is not required to make payments with respect to shrinkage payments (i.e. losses after goods have been delivered to the retailer’s premises resulting from theft, damage, internal accounting error, internal loss, or any other internal aspect of the retailer’s chain) unless the supplier was negligent. The supplier may however be required to assist the retailer in training in product knowledge and handling to reduce shrinkage. Additionally, unless as a result of the supplier’s negligence, a retailer is precluded from requiring a supplier to pay for compensation for forecasting errors; payments conditional for listing or stocking; or shelf positioning or increase in shelf space allocation.


  • Establishment of Committees

    The Code establishes two Committees; the Prompt Payment Committee and the Retail Trade Dispute Settlement Committee. The primary mandate of the Prompt Payment Committee is to assess the implementation of the Code while the Retail Trade Dispute Settlement Committee shall be the dispute resolution body for all disputes arising under the Code. Appeals from the decisions of the Retail Trade Settlement Committee regarding matters of abuse of Buyer Power shall lie to the Authority.

In conclusion, the Code is a necessary addition to the regulatory environment governing retailer-supplier relations given the recent history. It introduces a variety of obligations upon both retailers and suppliers, locally and, abroad and will require a reconsideration of existing supply agreements and arrangements to ensure compliance. All stakeholders are urged to adhere to the Code as well as the guidelines on Buyer Power as an indictment for abuse of Buyer Power results in a fine not exceeding KES 10 million or imprisonment for a term not exceeding 10 years or both.
